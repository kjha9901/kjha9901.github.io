Introduction¶

Air pollution is a significant environmental problem that affects the health and well-being of millions of people worldwide. Poor air quality has been linked to various health issues, including respiratory and cardiovascular diseases, as well as developmental and neurological problems. With the growing concern over the impact of air pollution on public health and safety, there has been an increasing interest in understanding the relationship between air quality and various related subjects, such as climate change, energy use, and transportation.

Using the data science pipeline, we analyze air quality data from multiple sources to draw conclusions about the impact of air pollution on human health and safety, as well as explore potential solutions to mitigate the negative effects of air pollution. By examining the trends in air quality over time and across different regions, I hope to provide insights that can inform and promote a cleaner and healthier environment for all.

The data science pipeline includes the following:

  1. Data Collection
  2. Data Processing/Management
  3. Exploratory Data Analysis
  4. Hypothesis Testing
  5. Conclusion

Data Collection¶

We will start by gathering the data that is relevant for our topic. In this case, we are looking for a dataset that contains information about the pollution levels across a number of years in a specific area. We also have to ensure the data collected is up to date and legitimate. To this end, the US government has a catalogue of data in which we can find our source: https://catalog.data.gov/dataset/air-quality. This is a dataset perfect for our purpose; New York City air quality surveillance data. The site includes a download of a .csv of the dataset, giving us direct access to the information.

We also need information about the health of the population during these years so we can actually find out ourselves if there is a correlation between health and air quality and how strong it might be. Our first dataset does have data for this, but in case we need additional data, the US government also has a dataset that gives us information on 21 selected chronic conditions among original medicare beneficiaries, of which includes ailments such as asthma, lung cancer, and heart failure, among others: https://catalog.data.gov/dataset/specific-chronic-conditions-0f448. While this dataset covers other states, we can separate out just the relevant information later during our data processing step.

We will be using Python to analyze the data. We will need to import relevant libraries for this purpose. We also need to import the actual .csv files containing the data we need. Below is the code that completes these tasks.

We can see by the graph that in general, the measurements have trended down on average over the years. However, this is not very useful as the data values measure many different things; we need to choose one specific measurement type to single out at least for now. If we don't, the graph will be irrelevant as it will be comparing datapoint that measure Ozone to datapoints that measure Nitrogen, for example, and a change in value for Ozone could be counteracted by a change in value for Nitrogen in the opposite direction, and we woulnd't we able to tell with out current graph. As such, let us pick "Fine Particulate Matter (PM2.5)" for now, as this seems to be a measurement that could negatively impact health if the value is high enough.
